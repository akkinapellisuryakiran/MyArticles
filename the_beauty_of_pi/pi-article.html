An Essay on the Infinite

It's just a number. And yet - it contains everything that has ever happened, or ever will.

3.14159265358979323846264338327950288...

Let me say something that sounds absurd on the surface, and I want you to sit with it before you dismiss it. The moment you just read the title of this piece - the way your eyes moved, the slight pause you took, the electrical signal that fired between your neurons - that entire sequence of events can be expressed as a number. And that number? It lives somewhere inside pi.

I know. Stay with me.

Pi - written as π, valued at roughly 3.14159 and then going on forever - is the ratio of a circle's circumference to its diameter. That's how most of us remember it. Something we memorized in school, maybe up to five or six decimal places, and then moved on with our lives. A math thing. A geometry thing. Not exactly the kind of thing that keeps you up at night wondering about the nature of existence.

Except it should.

"Pi doesn't repeat. It doesn't end. It never settles into a pattern. It is, quite literally, infinite chaos dressed up as a single clean symbol."

Here's what makes pi different from most numbers we deal with: it is irrational and almost certainly normal. Irrational means it cannot be expressed as a simple fraction - it has no final digit, no point at which it becomes a cycle. Normal (a technical term mathematicians haven't formally proven yet, but strongly believe) means every possible sequence of digits eventually shows up in pi - with equal frequency. Every combination. Every string of numbers you could ever imagine.

And that's where things get philosophically wild.

Everything That Exists Can Be Written as a Number

Think about what you're doing right now. You're reading. Your eyes scan left to right. Each letter on this screen is a character. Each character has an ASCII code - a number. The sentence "I am reading this article about pi" can be converted, letter by letter, into a precise string of digits. Your name can. Your face, digitized, can. The sound of your mother's voice. The memory of your first heartbreak. The exact way rain sounds when it hits a tin roof at 3am.

Anything that can be experienced, captured, or described - can ultimately be expressed as information. And information, at its base level, is a number. A very long one, sure. But a number nonetheless.

And the draft I wrote before it. And the version of this draft from a parallel universe where I used a different word in the third paragraph. Somewhere in pi is the number that represents you reading this sentence, right now, in this moment.

That's not poetry. That's mathematics, pushed to its logical edge.

How Pi Quietly Powers the World You Live In

But let's come back to Earth for a second, because pi isn't just a philosophical rabbit hole. It shows up everywhere, and I mean everywhere, in the technologies we use without thinking twice.

Real-World Example 01

Signal Processing & Music
Your music is made of pi

Every time you put on headphones, pi is working behind the scenes. The Fourier Transform - the mathematical engine that converts sound into digital audio files - is built on sine and cosine waves. Those waves are defined using π. When engineers compress a song into an MP3, they're essentially breaking that sound into circular wave components, measuring them in radians (which involve π), and reassembling them in your ear. The warmth of a vinyl record, the bass drop that hits your chest - all of it rides on pi.

Real-World Example 02

GPS & Space Navigation
NASA uses pi to talk to spacecraft billions of miles away

NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory literally publishes the number of pi digits they use for space calculations: 15. That's enough to calculate the circumference of a circle 25 billion miles across with an error smaller than the width of a hydrogen atom. When the Voyager probe is hurtling through interstellar space and scientists need to know exactly where it is and how to point an antenna at it from Earth - that calculation runs through π. It is baked into the geometry of the universe itself.

Real-World Example 03

Machine Learning & AI
Pattern recognition - the technology that reads your face - uses pi

The algorithms that power facial recognition, image compression, and neural network training all rely heavily on linear algebra and Fourier analysis. When a computer "learns" what a human face looks like by processing thousands of images, it's working with pixel grids, activation functions, and wave decompositions. Pi appears in the Gaussian distributions used to initialize neural weights, in the circular geometry of convolutional filters, and in the normalization functions that keep AI models stable. The thing that recognizes your face at the airport? Pi helped build it.

Real-World Example 04

Medicine & MRI Scans
The machine that looks inside your body speaks the language of π

MRI machines work by making hydrogen atoms in your body resonate - spin - inside a magnetic field, then measuring the radio waves they emit as they relax. The math that reconstructs those emissions into the image your doctor sees on a screen? Fourier transforms, again. Circular geometry. Pi. The slice-by-slice portrait of your brain, the scan that catches a tumor early enough to save your life - it was assembled using the same irrational, infinite number that never ends and never repeats.

"Pi is not a human invention. We discovered it. And in discovering it, we may have stumbled onto the syntax of the universe itself."

The Part That Should Actually Unsettle You

Here's what I keep returning to, personally. Pi is not something we created. It was always there, waiting in the relationship between a circle's edge and its width. Civilizations discovered it independently - the Babylonians, the Egyptians, Archimedes, Indian mathematicians. We didn't write it into existence. We just noticed it.

And it's everywhere a circle is. Which is everywhere. Orbits are circles. Waves are circular. DNA's double helix is a circular pattern spiraling through space. The iris of your eye. The cross-section of a blood vessel. The ripple a raindrop makes when it hits still water. Pi is in all of it.

So here we have this one number - irrational, non-repeating, infinite - that the physical world keeps insisting on. And buried inside that number, if we're right about it being "normal," is every sequence that has ever existed or ever will.

Every conversation you've had. Every dream you remember and every one you don't. The exact pattern of neurons that fired when you fell in love for the first time. The last words of people who lived thousands of years before you. Every book that was never written. Every song that was never recorded. Every life that was never lived.

Encoded, somewhere, in a number we've been calculating since before recorded history.

I'm not making a religious argument. I'm not making a mystical one. I'm making a mathematical one, which somehow ends up feeling like both.

A Personal Note - Why I Actually Care About This

I'll be honest: I didn't grow up loving math. Numbers felt cold and abstract to me for a long time - useful, sure, but not alive. Pi changed that. Not because of what it solves, but because of what it suggests.

It suggests that the universe has a kind of completeness to it. That everything fits, somewhere. That nothing is outside the system. And for whatever reason - maybe it's just the way my mind works - I find that deeply comforting. Even the chaos, the randomness, the moments that feel utterly formless and out of control - they're already in pi. Already accounted for. Already part of the pattern, even if the pattern has no pattern.

That's not nothing. That's actually kind of everything.

Life Goes On. And On. And On. And On…

You wake up. You make coffee. You argue with someone you love. You laugh at something stupid. You lose someone. You find someone. You start over. You try again. You fail differently this time. You get older. You get wiser, maybe. You look out a window at 2am and wonder what any of it means.

And then tomorrow comes. And you do it again.

Life doesn't stop to explain itself. It doesn't reach a conclusion, wrap up neatly, hand you a lesson with a bow on it. It just continues - moment after moment after moment, each one distinct, each one unrepeatable, the sequence never cycling back to exactly where it started.

"Pi doesn't end. Life doesn't end - not really. Both just keep going, carrying everything that ever happened inside them, never once repeating."

Sound familiar?

Pi is the same. 3.14159… and then on, and on, and on, and on - through billions of digits that have already been calculated, and trillions more that haven't been yet, and an infinity beyond that which never will be, because there isn't enough time in the universe to reach the end of a number that has no end.

That's not a flaw. That's the point.

We spend so much of our lives looking for the pattern - the moment things will finally make sense, the chapter where everything resolves. But maybe existence was never meant to resolve. Maybe it was meant to continue. To hold every version of you - the one you were at seven, at seventeen, at thirty-four - all at once, in an unbroken sequence that never repeats and never stops.

Pi doesn't apologize for being infinite. It doesn't try to become rational. It just keeps going - carrying the whole universe inside it, one digit at a time.

Maybe we're supposed to do the same.

Life goes on and on and on and on and on and on and on and on………

just like π.
